It has become a mandated outcome measure for clinical trials in RA, as well as a helpful tool in clinical settings for assessment and monitoring of RA. Have students access the HAQ on the Stanford University Medical Center website (see ‘Resources' below) and answer the following questions: How can the items in the HAQ be used to measure patient outcomes? The HAQ has been shown to be useful in predicting how well patients will do over the course of their disease and how well their disease symptoms are managed. As a health care provider, would you find this tool easy to administer? How could you use the information gained from the HAQ? The HAQ is designed to be completed by the patient as a self-report of their disease symptoms and functional abilities; it can also be administered by the provider during an interview. It is easily scored and provides a measureable way to serially monitor and follow patients with RA. Because the tool can help monitor patient symptoms and disease progression over time, providers can use the HAQ to help make informed decisions about treatment plans. How can using a consistent tool like the HAQ help provide quality and safe patient care? By using a reliable and valid tool for assessing functional outcomes, consistent treatment can be provided for patient populations with the same disease. HIV/AIDS prevention must take cultural context into account. Stanford University School of Education developed free materials that have been used around the world. TeachAIDS is a nonprofit organization that works to address and overcome the social and cultural challenges related to HIV/AIDS prevention education. TeachAIDS utilizes a rigorous research-based process to develop medically accurate, culturally tailored HIV/AIDS educational animations. In partnership with national and international organizations, and incorporating the voices of global cultural icons, TeachAIDS disseminates free animated curricula to educators around the world. TeachAIDS materials have been tested with tremendous success in numerous developing regions and are currently used in more than 25 countries, including South Africa, China, Canada, Botswana, India, and the United States.
